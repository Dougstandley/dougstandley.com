# The Last 5%

Executives don't reject AI because it's wrong 5% of the time. They reject it because they can't control which 5% — and that 5% is exactly where risk, value, and accountability live.

AI's prevailing narrative is about accuracy. Each quarter brings new benchmarks, new models, and new graphs trending up and to the right. The implicit promise: once the technology gets accurate enough, the market will open. Vendors pitch this, analysts chart it, and FOMO investors reward it. And in every deck: *a team of AI PhDs awaits collaboration on your next breakthrough.*

This framing makes sense for the people building AI. It has no purchase on the people buying it.

An executive evaluating AI for a regulated institution is not running a benchmark. They are running a risk calculation. They want to know what the system does when it is wrong. Not how often — that can be measured and disclosed — but where, how, and who pays when it happens. A model that is 99% accurate but fails unpredictably before a regulator, customer, or audit committee is not 99% useful. It is unusable, because the other 1% cannot be insured.

The industry measures averages. The buyer lives with the worst case.
